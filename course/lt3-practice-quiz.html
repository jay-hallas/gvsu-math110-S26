    Worksheet Learning Target 3: Linear Patterns
    

    Objectives

    • I can recognize and identify key characteristics of a linear pattern represented in a table, graph, equation, or application.
      🔗
    • I can translate between different representations.
      🔗
    🔗

    1. Is the Pattern Linear?

    Consider the following pattern of blocks.
    🔗

    (a)

    Explain why the pattern is linear.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The cases have \(6, 8, 10, 12, 14\) blocks. The count increases by the same amount, \(2\) blocks, from each case to the next. Because the rate of change is constant, the pattern is linear.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    How many blocks will be in case 8?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Continuing to add \(2\) each case: case 6 has \(16\) , case 7 has \(18\) , and case 8 has \(20\) blocks.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Find a linear equation that gives the number of blocks \(y\) for case \(x\) .
    🔗
    Answer.
    The number of blocks increases by \(2\) per case, so the slope is \(2\) . Working backward, case \(0\) would have \(4\) blocks, so the y-intercept is \(4\) . The equation is \(y = 2x + 4\) . Check: case \(3\) gives \(2(3)+4 = 10\) blocks, which matches.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Find the Equation of the Graph.

    Given the linear graph below, find an equation that matches it. Verify that your equation is correct by checking that it matches the line at two points.
    🔗
    (The points \((0,2)\) and \((4,4)\) are marked to help you.)
    🔗
    Answer.
    The line crosses the y-axis at \((0,2)\) , so \(b=2\) . From \((0,2)\) to \((4,4)\) the line rises \(2\) and runs \(4\) , so the slope is \(\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\) . The equation is \(y = \frac{1}{2}x + 2\) .
    🔗
    Check at \((0,2)\) : \(\frac{1}{2}(0)+2 = 2\) . Check at \((4,4)\) : \(\frac{1}{2}(4)+2 = 4\) . Both points match, so the equation is correct.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Could This Table Be Linear?

    Determine whether the following table could represent linear change. Explain your answer in writing.
    🔗
    \(x\) \(0\) \(1\) \(2\) \(3\) \(4\)
    \(y\) \(5\) \(10\) \(20\) \(40\) \(80\)
    Answer.
    It is not linear. As \(x\) increases by \(1\) each time, \(y\) changes by \(+5\) , then \(+10\) , then \(+20\) , then \(+40\) . The rate of change is not constant (the values double each step), so the table cannot represent linear change.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4. Could This Table Be Linear?

    Determine whether the following table could represent linear change. Explain your answer in writing.
    🔗
    \(x\) \(0\) \(1\) \(2\) \(3\) \(4\)
    \(y\) \(4\) \(10\) \(16\) \(22\) \(28\)
    Answer.
    It could be linear. As \(x\) increases by \(1\) each time, \(y\) increases by \(6\) every time. The rate of change is constant at \(6\) , which is the defining characteristic of a linear pattern. (An equation would be \(y = 6x + 4\) .)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5. Charging a Phone Battery.

    The following table gives the percent charge of a phone battery \(t\) minutes after it is plugged in.
    🔗
    \(t\) (minutes) \(0\) \(5\) \(10\) \(15\) \(20\)
    charge (%) \(20\) \(30\) \(40\) \(50\) \(60\)

    (a)

    The data appears linear. Find an equation for the battery percentage \(P\) after \(t\) minutes.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The charge increases by \(10\) percentage points every \(5\) minutes, a rate of \(2\) percent per minute. At \(t=0\) the charge is \(20\%\) . So \(P = 2t + 20\) .
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Suppose we later check the phone at \(t = 30\) minutes and find the charge is only \(74\%\) . Does this match the linear pattern, or does it suggest the charging may not always be linear? Explain your reasoning.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The linear equation predicts \(P = 2(30) + 20 = 80\%\) at \(t=30\) . The actual charge of \(74\%\) is lower than the linear prediction. This suggests the charging is not always linear: as a battery fills up, charging tends to slow down, so the percentage grows more slowly than the early linear rate would predict.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    6. Draining a Pool.

    The amount of water, in gallons, left in a pool \(t\) hours after it starts being emptied is given by the function \(W(t) = 16000 - 2000t\) .
    🔗

    (a)

    Make a table showing the water left after \(0\) , \(1\) , \(2\) , and \(3\) hours.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Substituting each value of \(t\) :
    🔗
    \(t\) (hours) \(0\) \(1\) \(2\) \(3\)
    \(W\) (gallons) \(16000\) \(14000\) \(12000\) \(10000\)
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    How much water did the pool start with?
    🔗
    Answer.
    At \(t=0\) , \(W(0) = 16000 - 2000(0) = 16000\) gallons. This is the y-intercept of the function.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    When will the pool be empty?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The pool is empty when \(W(t) = 0\) . Solving \(0 = 16000 - 2000t\) gives \(2000t = 16000\) , so \(t = 8\) . The pool will be empty after \(8\) hours.
    🔗
