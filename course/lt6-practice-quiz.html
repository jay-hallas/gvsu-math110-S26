    Worksheet Learning Target 6: Linear Regression Models
    

    Objectives

    • Given a data set, make a linear regression model on Desmos, identify and explain the meaning of slope in the context, identify and explain the y-intercept in the context, find \(f(a)\) and explain its meaning, find \(x\) given \(f(a)\) and explain its meaning.
      🔗
    🔗

    1. Latitude and Winter Temperature.

    The table below gives the latitude (degrees north) and the average January temperature (in \({}^\circ\)F) for ten U.S. cities.
    🔗
    City Latitude (\({}^\circ\)N) Avg. January temp (\({}^\circ\)F)
    Miami, FL 25.8 68.5
    Houston, TX 29.8 55.0
    Dallas, TX 32.8 48.0
    Atlanta, GA 33.7 45.0
    Memphis, TN 35.1 42.0
    St. Louis, MO 38.6 32.0
    Cincinnati, OH 39.1 31.5
    Indianapolis, IN 39.8 28.5
    Chicago, IL 41.9 26.5
    Minneapolis, MN 45.0 16.5
    Source: average January temperature is the mean of the 1991–2020 NOAA normal daily high and low (NOAA National Centers for Environmental Information, via Current Results); latitudes are the cities’ coordinates.
    🔗

    (a)

    What are the independent and dependent variables in this situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Independent variable: \(L\text{,}\) latitude (degrees north). Dependent variable: \(T\text{,}\) average January temperature (\({}^\circ\)F).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Create a linear model that represents the data. Round numbers to 3 decimal places on your paper.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Entering the data in Desmos and fitting a line gives \(T = -2.633L + 134.561\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    According to your model, what is the average January temperature for a city at latitude \(43^\circ\)N? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(L = 43\text{:}\) \(T = -2.633(43) + 134.561 = 21.342\text{,}\) or about \(21^\circ\)F.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    According to your model, at what latitude is the average January temperature \(40^\circ\)F? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(T = 40\) and solve for \(L\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(40 = -2.633L + 134.561\)
    🔗
    \(-94.561 = -2.633L\) (subtract \(134.561\) from both sides)
    🔗
    \(L = 35.914\) (divide both sides by \(-2.633\))
    🔗
    So the model predicts an average January temperature of \(40^\circ\)F at about \(36^\circ\)N latitude.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    What is the slope in the equation, and what does it mean in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The slope is \(-2.633\text{.}\) It means that for each additional degree of latitude north, the average January temperature decreases by about \(2.633^\circ\)F.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    What is the vertical intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The vertical intercept is \((0, 135)\text{.}\) This says that at latitude \(0^\circ\) (the equator), the average January temperature would be about \(135^\circ\)F. This does not make sense: it is far outside the range of the data, and no place on Earth averages \(135^\circ\)F.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (g)

    What is the horizontal intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(T = 0\text{:}\) \(0 = -2.633L + 134.561\text{,}\) so \(L = 51.106\text{.}\) The horizontal intercept is about \((51, 0)\text{,}\) meaning the model predicts an average January temperature of \(0^\circ\)F at about \(51^\circ\)N. This is just beyond the data, and nothing about it is obviously impossible. Far-northern cities do have very cold Januaries, so a prediction near \(0^\circ\)F is at least plausible. Keep in mind, though, that it is not accurate for every city near that latitude. Because the model uses only latitude, it misses other factors (such as nearness to an ocean), so cities around \(51^\circ\)N actually range from quite cold to fairly mild.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. Height and Arm Span.

    The table below gives the height and arm span, both in centimeters, for ten students.
    🔗
    Height (cm) Arm span (cm)
    154 158
    157 157
    161 162
    164 163
    167 172
    168 161
    173 172
    177 181
    185 184
    191 190
    Source: measured data from a sample of 25 students (SERC / Carleton College example data set); ten of the students are listed here.
    🔗

    (a)

    What are the independent and dependent variables in this situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Independent variable: \(h\text{,}\) height (cm). Dependent variable: \(s\text{,}\) arm span (cm).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Create a linear model that represents the data. Round numbers to 3 decimal places on your paper.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Entering the data in Desmos and fitting a line gives \(s = 0.939h + 10.670\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    According to your model, what is the arm span of a student who is \(180\) cm tall? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(h = 180\text{:}\) \(s = 0.939(180) + 10.670 = 179.690\text{,}\) or about \(180\) cm.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    According to your model, how tall is a student whose arm span is \(175\) cm? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(s = 175\) and solve for \(h\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(175 = 0.939h + 10.670\)
    🔗
    \(164.330 = 0.939h\) (subtract \(10.670\) from both sides)
    🔗
    \(h = 175.005\) (divide both sides by \(0.939\))
    🔗
    So the model predicts a height of about \(175\) cm.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    What is the slope in the equation, and what does it mean in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The slope is \(0.939\text{.}\) It means that for each additional centimeter of height, arm span increases by about \(0.939\) cm—close to the "arm span equals height" rule of thumb.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    What is the vertical intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The vertical intercept is \((0, 11)\text{.}\) This says a student who is \(0\) cm tall would have an arm span of about \(11\) cm. This does not make sense, because no one has a height of \(0\) cm.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (g)

    What is the horizontal intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(s = 0\text{:}\) \(0 = 0.939h + 10.670\text{,}\) so \(h = -11.363\text{.}\) The horizontal intercept is about \((-11, 0)\text{,}\) meaning an arm span of \(0\) cm at a height of \(-11\) cm. This does not make sense, because a person cannot have a negative height.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. Runs and Wins in Baseball.

    The table below gives the total runs scored and total wins for ten Major League Baseball teams during the 2025 regular season (each team played 162 games).
    🔗
    Team Runs scored Wins
    Pittsburgh Pirates 583 71
    Washington Nationals 687 66
    St. Louis Cardinals 689 78
    Cincinnati Reds 716 83
    Atlanta Braves 724 76
    New York Mets 766 83
    Philadelphia Phillies 778 96
    Chicago Cubs 793 92
    Milwaukee Brewers 806 97
    Los Angeles Dodgers 825 93
    Source: 2025 MLB regular-season team totals—runs scored from FOX Sports (Sportradar); wins from the final 2025 MLB standings.
    🔗

    (a)

    What are the independent and dependent variables in this situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Independent variable: \(r\text{,}\) runs scored. Dependent variable: \(W\text{,}\) wins.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Create a linear model that represents the data. Round numbers to 3 decimal places on your paper.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Entering the data in Desmos and fitting a line gives \(W = 0.125r - 8.618\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    According to your model, how many games would a team that scores \(750\) runs win? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(r = 750\text{:}\) \(W = 0.125(750) - 8.618 = 85.132\text{,}\) or about \(85\) wins.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    According to your model, how many runs would a team need to score to win \(90\) games? Round your answer to a whole number.
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(W = 90\) and solve for \(r\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \(90 = 0.125r - 8.618\)
    🔗
    \(98.618 = 0.125r\) (add \(8.618\) to both sides)
    🔗
    \(r = 788.944\) (divide both sides by \(0.125\))
    🔗
    So the model predicts a team would need to score about \(789\) runs to win \(90\) games.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (e)

    What is the slope in the equation, and what does it mean in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The slope is \(0.125\text{.}\) It means that for each additional run a team scores over the season, the model predicts about \(0.125\) more wins—so roughly \(8\) extra runs for each additional win.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (f)

    What is the vertical intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The vertical intercept is \((0, -9)\text{.}\) This says a team that scores \(0\) runs all season would win about \(-9\) games. This does not make sense, because a team cannot win a negative number of games (and \(0\) runs is far outside the data).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (g)

    What is the horizontal intercept (round to a whole number) and explain what it means (or why it doesn’t make sense) in the context of the situation?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Let \(W = 0\text{:}\) \(0 = 0.125r - 8.618\text{,}\) so \(r = 68.944\text{.}\) The horizontal intercept is about \((69, 0)\text{,}\) meaning a team scoring about \(69\) runs would win \(0\) games. This does not make sense: it is far below the data (even the weakest teams score around \(580\) runs), and such a team would still win at least a few games.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
