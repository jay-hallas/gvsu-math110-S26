    Worksheet Learning Target 2: Reading Qualitative Graphs
    

    Objectives

    • I can describe a situation given a qualitative graph with defined inputs and outputs.
      🔗
    🔗

    1. Heart Rate During a Workout.

    The graph shows a runner’s heart rate during a workout, from the moment they begin warming up until a few minutes after they finish.
    🔗
    
    A graph with time in minutes on the horizontal axis (0 to 40) and heart rate in beats per minute on the vertical axis (0 to 200). The line begins at 60 beats per minute, rises to 100 by 5 minutes, and stays flat at 100 from 5 to 10 minutes. It then climbs to 160 by 15 minutes and stays flat at 160 from 15 to 25 minutes. After 25 minutes it drops more quickly to 120 by 30 minutes, then declines gradually to 80 by 40 minutes.
    🔗

    (a)

    Identify the independent variable (the input) and the dependent variable (the output) for this graph, including their units.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The independent variable (input) is the time, measured in minutes, shown on the horizontal axis. The dependent variable (output) is the heart rate, measured in beats per minute (bpm), shown on the vertical axis. The heart rate depends on how much time has passed, so heart rate is a function of time.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Over what period of time is the runner exercising most intensely, and what is the highest heart rate they reached during the workout?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The runner is working hardest where the heart rate is highest, which is the upper flat stretch from \(15\) to \(25\) minutes. The highest heart rate reached is \(160\) bpm, and they hold it there for about \(10\) minutes.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    During which interval(s) is the heart rate staying constant? What is the runner most likely doing during each of those times?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The graph is horizontal in two places, so the heart rate is constant there. From \(5\) to \(10\) minutes it holds steady at \(100\) bpm, an easy effort during the warm-up. From \(15\) to \(25\) minutes it holds steady at \(160\) bpm, the hard, steady part of the workout. A flat (horizontal) graph means the heart rate is neither rising nor falling.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    Describe what happens to the heart rate after \(25\) minutes. What is the runner doing, and is their heart rate back to where it started by the end of the graph?
    🔗
    Answer.
    After \(25\) minutes the heart rate drops fairly quickly from \(160\) to \(120\) bpm (by \(30\) minutes), then declines more gradually to \(80\) bpm at \(40\) minutes. The runner has finished the hard effort and is cooling down, so the heart slows back toward its resting rate. It has not returned to the starting value yet: the graph begins at \(60\) bpm but ends at \(80\) bpm, so the runner is still recovering at the end.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    2. A Savings Account.

    The graph shows the balance in a student’s savings account over a ten-week period. The input is the time in weeks and the output is the balance in dollars.
    🔗
    
    A graph with time in weeks on the horizontal axis (0 to 10) and balance in dollars on the vertical axis (0 to 500). The line starts at 100 dollars at week 0 and rises steadily to 300 dollars at week 4. It is flat from week 4 to week 5. It then drops sharply to 100 dollars at week 6, and finally rises steadily back to 300 dollars at week 10.
    🔗

    (a)

    What was the balance at the start of the ten weeks? What does this starting value (the vertical intercept) tell you?
    🔗
    Answer.
    At week \(0\) the balance is \(\$100\) . The vertical intercept \((0,100)\) is the amount of money already in the account when the ten-week period began.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    During which week does the balance go down, and what does that tell you about the story?
    🔗
    Answer.
    Between week \(5\) and week \(6\) the balance falls from \(\$300\) to \(\$100\) , a decrease of \(\$200\) . A segment that goes downward means money was taken out, so the student likely made a large purchase that week.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Compare how fast the balance is growing during weeks \(0\) to \(4\) with how fast it grows during weeks \(6\) to \(10\) . How can you tell from the graph?
    🔗
    Answer.
    In both stretches the balance climbs from \(\$100\) to \(\$300\) , a gain of \(\$200\) over \(4\) weeks, or \(\$50\) per week. The two rising segments have the same steepness, so the student is saving at the same steady rate in both stretches. (The steepest segment overall is the drop from week \(5\) to week \(6\) , but there the balance is decreasing, not growing.)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3. A Mountain Hike.

    The graph shows your elevation as you walk along a hiking trail. Note: the input here is the distance you have hiked, in miles, not the time. The output is your elevation in feet.
    🔗
    
    A graph with distance in miles on the horizontal axis (0 to 6) and elevation in feet on the vertical axis (0 to 1000). The line starts at 200 feet at mile 0 and rises to 600 feet at mile 2. It is flat from mile 2 to mile 3. It then climbs steeply to 1000 feet at mile 4, and finally descends steadily back to 200 feet at mile 6.
    🔗

    (a)

    Over which interval are you climbing most steeply? How does the graph show this?
    🔗
    Answer.
    From mile \(3\) to mile \(4\) the elevation rises from \(600\) to \(1000\) feet, a rate of \(400\) feet per mile. From mile \(0\) to mile \(2\) it rises only \(200\) feet per mile. The segment from \(3\) to \(4\) is the steepest uphill part, so you climb most steeply there. The steeper the upward line, the faster you are gaining elevation.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Where is the trail flat? What does that mean for your hike, even though you keep walking forward?
    🔗
    Answer.
    From mile \(2\) to mile \(3\) the graph is horizontal at \(600\) feet, so your elevation does not change. You are walking along level ground (a flat ridge): you keep moving forward along the trail, but you are neither climbing nor descending.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Compare your elevation at the end of the hike with where you started. What does the graph tell you?
    🔗
    Answer.
    You start at \((0,200)\) and end at \((6,200)\) , both at \(200\) feet. The trail climbs to a high point of \(1000\) feet at mile \(4\) and then descends. So even though you hiked \(6\) miles and gained a lot of elevation along the way, you finish at the same height as the trailhead where you began.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    4. Charging a Phone Battery.

    The graph shows the charge of a phone battery over time. The phone starts at \(20\%\text{,}\) is plugged in to charge, and is later unplugged and used.
    🔗
    
    A graph with time in minutes on the horizontal axis (0 to 50) and battery charge as a percent on the vertical axis (0 to 100). Starting at 20 percent, the curve rises quickly and then flattens as it climbs, reaching about 90 percent at 30 minutes. After 30 minutes the graph turns downward: it declines gently from 90 percent to 80 percent between 30 and 45 minutes, then declines more steeply from 80 percent to 60 percent between 45 and 50 minutes.
    🔗

    (a)

    Identify the independent variable (the input) and the dependent variable (the output) for this graph, including their units.
    🔗
    Answer.
    The independent variable (input) is the time, measured in minutes, shown on the horizontal axis. The dependent variable (output) is the battery charge, measured as a percent, shown on the vertical axis. The charge depends on how much time has passed, so charge is a function of time.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    For how long was the phone plugged in and charging, and when was it unplugged? How does the graph show this?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The charge rises from \(0\) to \(30\) minutes, so the phone was charging for \(30\) minutes. It was unplugged at \(30\) minutes: that is the highest point on the graph (about \(90\%\)), where the charge stops rising and begins to fall.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    The charging part of the graph is curved, not a straight line. What does its shape tell you about how the battery charges?
    🔗
    Answer.
    The curve is steep at first and flattens as it rises, so the charge increases quickly at the start and more slowly as the battery fills. In other words, the battery charges fastest when it is nearly empty and slows down as it approaches full. Because the curve is not straight, the rate of charging is not constant—each extra percent takes a little longer than the one before.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    (d)

    After the phone is unplugged, when is the battery draining the fastest? What is happening to the phone then, compared with the time just after it was unplugged?
    🔗
    Answer.
    From \(45\) to \(50\) minutes the graph falls most steeply, dropping from \(80\%\) to \(60\%\)—about \(4\%\) per minute. That is when a video or app is running and using power quickly. Just after being unplugged, from \(30\) to \(45\) minutes, the phone sat mostly idle and lost only about \(0.7\%\) per minute, so that part of the graph is much less steep.
    🔗
